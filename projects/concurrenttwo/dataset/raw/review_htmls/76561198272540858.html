I love Outriders. LOVE it. I'm over 100 hours in and having a blast. I've played it solo, 2-player, and 3-player and it works each way (though the net code does still need a little polish).

The key to enjoying Outriders is to know what it IS. It's a ARPG like Diablo or POE, but played like a 3rd-Person shooter. This means your gear, passive tree, and selected skills have a huge impact on how you play and how effective you are. It's not just bigger numbers you want, you actually need to care about how each component of your build works together. It is absolutely possible to make builds that can't succeed at the highest levels... and I, as a fan of ARPGs, like finding fun builds.

Despite a bunch of patches, Outriders still has some rough edges. The graphics feel like they're from 2016, the netcode does need more polish, the story is a little clunky at times, and if you try playing it purely as a cover shooter, you're just not going to *get* it. But despite these shortcomings, Outriders remains exceptional in a very literal sense of the word. I've played no other third (or first) person shooter capture the ARPG build magic the way Outriders does and for that reason alone it comes highly recommended to fans of the genre and fans of co-op gaming.

This game looks like it was made 15 years ago on a potato, but that doesn't stop it from being one of the best zombie survival games I've ever played. The secret to surviving the apocalypse is careful resource management, which includes both disposable resources (iron, wood, leather, etc.) and time (used for traveling, resting, building, and exploring). I'm not quite 10 hours in yet, but already I'm super impressed by how well these elements are balanced. Just when you think you're getting ahead of the curve, you realize you're out of wood and can no longer repair your fence to keep the zombies out at night. You'll wonder what you can do about and also what you could have done differently in the past.

The primary activity of the game is exploring, where you'll be wandering zombie infested structures for anything that might be useful. Dealing with the zombies is mechanically simple, for the most part, but surprisingly nuanced. You can use melee weapons to take them out quietly (risking personal injury), guns if you want to save time and protect yourself (but risk attracting the attention of other zombies), and pure stealth (if you've got the time and skill to bait out enemy zombies). It all works really well, despite being incredibly simple to control.

In addition to the obvious rough edges (which include the translation and overall production values), the game stumbles now and again. The first boss fight is a complete surprise that you can be completely unable to beat if you didn't come prepared, which is a bit of a bad feeling. There are aspects of the UI which are clunky to the point of being cumbersome. And there are some systems that are more than a bit arcane (making some learnings more painful that they should be, even in a game of this nature).

However, none of these problems get in the way of the core experience being a blast. If you're looking for a zombie survival game with an emphasis on survival, do not dismiss this game because of it's dated presentation. It's a great little title. The designers should be proud!

Not gonna lie, Rage is a bit of a bafflement to me.

The general AI is above average, the shooting competent, the animations solid, looting is always fun, and the voice acting is pretty good. But everything else is bafflingly bland or just downright bad.

There's more invisible walls in this game than I've encountered in any game in the passed decade, which gets even weirder in a psuedo-open world like this one. Yes, weapons are solid, but extremely limiting and uninspired. Enemy variety is shockingly limited (even when they change up skins they don't do much for behavior). Level is design is often downright poor, often corridors with some cover and limited maneuverability. The driving is awkward (you can't even use the mouse to look around when driving, it's a left-hand experience).

And then there's the art. The art is the brownest game that ever browned. Not only are environments mostly a hideous shade of washed out brownish-grey, but so are most of the enemies, making them eye-straining to spot for no reason in many situations. Foliage looks like it's out of the original Doom engine, as opposed to a modern title. Textures are so vacuous that even at mid range I often can't tell what I'm looking at. When I'm up close it's nearly impossible to tell if I'm looking at a sink or a desk. Why? WHY?

Rage isn't all bad, but when the death that erases 15 minutes of progress is due to a collision bug that instantly kills my character when riding an elevator, it's time to put the game down.

The Surge is probably the best Souls game out there that isn't from From Software. That's basically all you need to know, but if you're interested in more...

The Surge is a science fiction action adventure game with difficult combat, where gauging enemy attack patterns and managing stamina (used for both evasive maneuvers and attacking) is the key to victory. While wandering a dystopian industrial complex, you'll level up your "Exosuit," allowing you to wield increasingly more powerful mods and (more importantly) more *combinations* of mods. Mods can do everything from heal you during combat to increase your attack speed, making them a clever substitute for traditional stat progression found in similar games. All the while you'll discover new weapons and harvest armor from your defeated foes (which you do by hacking off their body parts and claiming their armor for your own, one piece at a time).

The game has flaws. Some zones are too dark, some enemy attacks not well telegraphed, and enemy/boss/environment variety doesn't hold a candle to the Souls games. However, it gets the basics more right than any other attempt at the formula outside of Dark Souls or Bloodborne themselves. Combat is satisfying and visceral, there are interesting build possibilities, and what bosses they do have range from decent to excellent.

A special call-out for the story. Unlike Dark Souls, which remains predominantly arcane from beginning to end (that is: you've got to be looking for the story to find it), The Surge gradually moves the story line to the forefront. Even though the ending itself leaves a little to be desired, the final act still manages some surprising emotional notes. At least two story lines sucker-punched me pretty good, which was a pleasant surprise.

If you haven't played Dark Souls, there's something wrong with you. Go play that. If you have and you're looking for something that gets the basics right and adds a few twists to the formula, you're not going to do much better than The Surge.
