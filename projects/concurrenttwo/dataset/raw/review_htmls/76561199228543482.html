Metro Exodus is an extremely story-rich post-nuclear first person RPG that spans several drastically different environments in utterly inhospitable climates. You'll find yourself armed with everything from tin cans and bb guns to shotguns, assault rifles, and molotovs. You can take the game at whatever pace you like, and the game will not artificially slow you down, but you will miss that almost every friend, ally, foe, bystander or decayed burnt corpse, had thoughts, aspirations, interests, family, kinks, and problems of their own. The people are complex; you can be offensive and shoot first, defensive and kill only who threaten to impede you, or maybe you can find another path altogether. There really are no rules, only karma. And radiation, wolves, bears, slugs, spiders, alcohol, communism, tentacle monsters, bandits, slavers, pirates, politicians, yetis, pterodactyls, your stupid father-in-law, and smoking cause lung-cancer is bad. I couldnt get input to work when enabling DX12, but I didnt feel like I could tell a difference in the visual quality in screenshots and videos between DX11 and DX12, with or without RTX. With an RTX 2070 Super, it looked and ran absolutely amazing anyway.

Even thought this game was originally released in 2016, it still receives updates and bug fixes, and notably works great with the Valve Index Knuckles controllers and holds up graphically. The developers are quite responsive to the community in the forums. Although it seems like it was made entirely from stock assets that more than not feel out of place and used without variation throughout the game and DLCs, this didnt bother me too much as the main game play was consistently fun. The game is entirely linear and doesnt reward exploration save for a handful of times in the DLCs, but sometimes you have to look around a bit to figure out what to do next. There are loads of different weapons, and availability of ammo means you will have to switch it up every once in a while. There is disappointingly no melee. The main game is narrated well by the protagonist. I would say it's an indie VR somewhere in between Alan Wake and Postal. I only played single player and barely touched the horde modes.

I was really impressed by how polished the gameplay is. The controls (with Valve Knuckles) are great and even considerately customizable. The gameplay is varied, thought out, and fun. There are quite a welcome amount of various enemies, minor puzzles of different types, scattered loot to horde, secrets to solve, pets to pet, and NPCs to abuse. There are quite a handful of weapons available, each with a completely different mechanic and style, and you can easily obtain them all or just settle on your favorite. Much of this interactivity felt like it went to waste though, as the entire game campaign went past far too fast, even though I got almost nine hours out of it. Others seem to be reporting about six. I deeply hope one or more expansions are released someday soon.

These guys should buy out Bethesda and only keep the writers. This game accentuates just how shameful Skyrim VR is.
