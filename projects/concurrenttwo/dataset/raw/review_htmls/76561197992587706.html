Early Impressions after about 45 hours.

First, Elden Ring is absolutely not the 10/10 masterpiece that it's being lauded as. From a general audience perspective, it lacks the versatile and complete package required for me to consider something a perfect game.

If you already love Dark Souls, however, then this game may just be the perfect iteration of that formula. If you're a Souls fan, then this is a no-brain must-buy.

I'm simply not a huge fan of this formula.

Even though the marketing for Elden Ring is aimed at a more general audience, and it touts legendary fantasy author George RR Martin, the game is really just open-world Dark Souls.

While there is an initial phase of gawking awe at the genuinely beautiful Gothic-style art design, there is a real lack of content diversity here. Just to put a fine point on this before I move on, I really do love the sweeping vistas, the detailed enemy designs, and the breadth of this open world. It's mesmerizing at times.

That's about where the variety ends.

The artificial difficulty starts rearing its head rather quickly on this uneven journey. Like previous Souls games, you'll find an obtuse, almost non-existent narrative with bland characterizations and a reliance on vague story beats to guide you towards becoming the "Elden Lord." This design philosophy bleeds over into virtually every aspect of the game.

Souls fans will tell you that not having any modern features makes the game "hardcore" and "not for the casuals." Really, all this achieves is inflating the playtime and manufacturing unnecessary grind and confusion. Don't expect anything like quests or logs or tooltips. Every single thing is buried in a clunky menu system with an ancient UI that I could see fitting right in to an early 2000's RPG.

Instead, Elden Ring's primary gameplay loop mostly involves riding your trusty horse-goat to different Dark Souls levels in whatever way you see fit, avoiding most enemies along the way while clicking Y on plants and stuff. It's a pretty boring loop, except for the pretty views. Along the way, you may discover little mini-dungeons and mini-bosses that you can complete for gear upgrades.

Your standard "Souls" system of grinding is back and in full effect. Every enemy you fell provides you with "runes" (souls) that you can redeem for levels and gear. Campfires (now called Sites of Grace) plentifully exist around the map allowing you to upgrade your potions, gear, and stats.

If that sounds familiar, that's because it's Dark Souls again. If that sounds awesome to you, chances are you don't need this review.

For me, this gameplay is a decent take on a stale formula that prioritizes min-maxing, stat-crunching, mob grinding, and boss fights, with little more to offer.

The combat is still clunky, and it relies on pitch-perfect timing and finding a rhythm around its constant animation-locking. The associated targeting system also needs work. It's entirely based on the direction of your character's face. So, if you are standing sideways, but have your camera facing the enemy, prepared to have the game whip the camera around a full 90 degrees away from what you're looking at and target something way off in the distance while the enemy right next to you one-shots you.

It's unforgiving, true. It's also often poorly designed and executed. Whether it's the unpredictable hit boxes on enemies, the inexplicable range of some attacks, the weapons glitching through walls and floors to hit you, you'll find frustratingly clunky mechanics all over this ancient combat system.

But, what about the bosses? That's the Souls bread-and-butter!

I'm happy to report that the boss fights are still the best part of these games. They're extremely rewarding while never compromising on challenge. The boss art-designs are truly magnificent as well. They feel beefy and dangerous. Once you finally discover a boss area, and sometimes the perfect route to get there, it definitely provides a feeling of achievement. Once you actually defeat that boss (which could take a dozen or more tries depending on your current power level), you feel a rush of accomplishment and adrenaline.

This aspect is so thoroughly satisfying at times that it makes coming back to the game less of a chore.

But, then there's the performance issues.

What a bust. I've recorded nine crashes in 45 hours. Five of those crashes needed complete system restarts. I've also experienced a fair amount of frame dips and light stutters. I've also had the game completely freeze several times in the middle of a boss battle that caused me to die and have to retry.

Thank God I don't mind playing games with a controller too, since the keyboard/mouse support for this game is laughable at best.

Overall, Elden Ring is very similar to other Dark Souls games for me. Yes it has a gorgeous open world, but there's very little actual variety to your activities or destinations. I would compare it to Cyberpunk 2077 in that regard. It has a ton of pretty window dressing, but underneath there just isn't much there. Gather, grind, fight boss, loot, get key, repeat.

Coupled with a lack of narrative, an over-reliance on artificial difficulty, a host of performance problems, and a general lack of immersive game-play, I'm currently sitting at a 6.5/10 for Elden Ring (still above average). I cautiously recommend it to general audiences for its impressive art design and fun, challenging bosses. I wholeheartedly recommend it for you masochists out there that love Dark Souls.

The marketing was pretty bogus for this game. Don't expect Breath of the Wild or Witcher 3. Don't expect any kind of real story or immersion. You won't get any epic RR Martin political intrigue. Instead, if you expect more Dark Souls with a much bigger map, then you will probably have a good time.
