This game is being metabombed because of Square Enix's shady DLC model. Essentially, the minor DLC (like the Assault Pack or the Praxis Kits) are one time use only. You access your inventory in game, and you transfer the item from the storage tab into your inventory. If you lose the save it's gone, and if you start a new game the content is gone.

I wish to separate the review of the physical, final product from the DLC/Store content which, IMO, is optional and not required in any way whatsoever. Yes, the model is shady and deplorable behavior by Square Enix, however, the actual game itself is a very good and enjoyable experience.

If you love the franchise but are hesitant about the DLC model, purchase the game and avoid the store entirely. Square Enix (if they're smart) differentiates between micro-transaction sales and physical/digital game copy sales. Vote with your wallet against the store and not the game.

Performance / Technicals

For starters, my PC is extremely powerful, so your mileage may vary. Under the hood I have 2 980 Tis, an i7 4970k processor, and 32 gbs of RAM. It's probably overkill for this game, because I experienced no major issues and maintained a solid FPS of 60 with most settings maxed. There were some dips here and there but they were brief, and it never went below 40 fps.

The mouse sensitivity is a little high (my mouse's inherent DPI is set to 800) so you're gonna want to reduce it quite a bit in-game. Some users have reported Mouse Acceleration, however I experienced no such thing.

Hint: Disabled MSAA and go with Temporal AA. I believe MSAA is disabled by default when you first start the game.

Besides that there's nothing really to say. For a console -> PC port this is a pretty smooth experience. My favorite bit is that for users with Chroma keyboards, the game has inherent settings built in for your keyboard colors. Loading screens have the keyboard doing a cycled wave of gold color. Very nice touch.

Story

As far as story goes, it's relatively uninspired and very 'safe.' You assume the role of Adam Jensen again, 2 years after the events of Human Revolution. The golden age of human augmentation has come to an abrupt halt in light of the 'Aug Incident,' when millions of augs (augmented humans) went rampant and killed naturals (humans without augs). Mankind Divided's backdrop essentially captures the growing tension between augs and naturals. This is reflected in the environment where you'll see augs getting bullied by zealous police.

You essentially run around trying to stop an Augmentation Right's Group from committing a major terrorist attack. It's a fairly standard story, but the biggest issue is that it sort of just ends suddenly after a relatively anti-climactic boss fight. It gives credence to Jim Sterling's original report from January that detailed the possibility that the bean counting suits/upper management at Square Enix HQ had requested the game be spliced up into several parts.

This becomes massively transparent during the game where they hint at certain locations, specifically Tai Yong Medical corporation which has a laboratory in San Francisco.

Finally, the game only has a single hub this time around (Prague), however the city is massive and very well-detailed. There are a lot of apartments/open interiors that you may enter and explore for items and loot. The sheer openness of the city also allows for the side-quests to have a more open nature to them (i.e. you can use the environment of the city to achieve objectives differently). You'll still visit various areas like a Ghetto on the outskirts of prague called Golem City, a place where augs are deported to. You'll also visit the Swiss Alps *very* briefly for a quest.

Gameplay

I find the gameplay to be a step above Human Revolution. The cover to cover mechanic has been improved greatly and is far more fluid than before. Gunplay, stealth, etc, all feel very satisfying. There are a few new augments that have been added, but for the most part you don't really need them. You can easily complete the game using the tried and true augments.

There's a new crafting system which lets you create your own items such as Mine templates, biocells (your energy source for augments), and multi-tools (yes they've returned). Multi-tools are my personal favorite because they allow you to quickly hack through computers/keypads when you're in a hurry and need to sneak by a guard patrol. Definitely a great addition to the game.

There are a few choices you can make in the game, however they aren't that impactful. The last mission, for example, had you choose between saving UN member's from being poisoned or fighting the last boss immediately, otherwise he'd blow up an adjacent skyscraper full of innocents. The strange thing about that segment is that I was able to save all the parties involved, so my choice ended up not being all that important. It could have also been the order in which I chose to do it.

Lastly, the alternative routes/sneaky gameplay we've all come to admire in the Deus Ex franchise is intact in Mankind Divided. There are tons of routes/methods to completing a particular objective. I also recommend that player's get the CASIE augment very early on. It will help them out in a lot of situations.

User Interface / Aesthetics / Art

The one thing that stood out to me with this game was how much better the user interface was. Everything is a lot more seamless, and icons/notifications are much more reactive/contextual to the player's actions. A huge step up from Human Revolution.

The big thing Human Revolution players will notice going into Mankind Divided is the major shift in tone and color. Human Revolution had a black/gold filter which helped establish one thing: The augment renaissance (gold). Mankind Divided has mostly dialed back the color palette and gone with more natural colors. I feel that this was largely done to serve as a visual cue to the player that the renaissance era of augments was over - and it most certainly was.

The gold/black filter does return, surprisingly enough, during the very last mission (which takes place at night). This is also another reason why the Gold/Black filter worked so well in Human Revolution. The game took place mostly at night, and so you had a natural build up of yellows/golds from street lights constrasted against the black night sky.

Verdict

Mankind Divided is a *good* game. There are still some areas that can be improved, but as a sequel to Human Revolution and a continuation of Adam Jensen's story arc, I have to say that I am overall very pleased with it. If you can look past the DLC model and look at the game itself, I know for a fact that you'll probably enjoy it as much as I did.
