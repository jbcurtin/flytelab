What type of game is it?
It's essentially a top down (not actually a dungeon) crawler style game, with survival elements. You arrive washed up on this mysterious island, and explore a large, mysterious area that exists below the surface. You must avoid death, which can be happen when you starve, get too cold, get caught in a trap, or from the various enemies in the game. Enemies aren't overwhelming like in real dungeon crawlers, however you has basic combat skills you can utilise to safely dispose of them. You can collect some resources and come across camp fires, that allow you to replenish your thirst, hunger, warmth and health. There's melee combat and ranged combat too, with some minor crafting elements for food and supplies. Its pretty dark down there, so you'll likely be crafting torches.... Oh one more thing - death is a big deal. When you die, you will start back off sailing onto the island. You lose your inventory, though if you can make it back to your corpse before dying - you can retrieve it again.

What did I love about it?
It was really refreshing for many reasons. There was almost 0 hand holding. A very minimal UI, no popups or tutorials. Instead, you discover how the game works. A lot of the UI is just icons and you kind of work out what things are (in a good way). There's even a couple pieces of armor you can find in the game, yet it doesn't tell you what it does - though you may guess or work out how it works. The game is very mysterious. You turn up on a ship to this island and have no idea why. The art style is great, sets amazing atmosphere and I really wanted to find out more about this place I was in. The music also is fantastic and really adds to the mood of the game - its by The game is pretty punishing, rewards careful and deliberate play with its difficulty. The darkness, survival elements, and risk of death play a major role in building up tension and emotion in you as a player. There are so many campfires you can use in the game, for example. Food isn't always easy to come by. These things add a bit of gentle pressure to your gameplay, forcing you to need to sometimes rush in a game the encourages careful and calculated play. Personally, I really liked the story - albeit being very abstract and no script or words used to convey it.

Early Access Review
I've had a lot of fun with this game. I foresee this game being as enjoyable to play as the first Rogue Legacy once the content has been completed. It feels and looks solid, controls well, is challenging and enjoyable. If you are new to RL, this will be a preview but you won't yet get the complete picture of what makes RL so wonderful. This will come when the content is fleshed out, as that will greatly increase the sense of progression you're making along with providing the ever increasing challenge.

Should you buy this?
If you plan on playing the finished version in the future and / or you want a taste of what's to come, give it a go! However, as it is EA you won't get a full feeling for the games challenge yet. I got about 10 hours gameplay out of this, and still am enjoying trying to beat the current content in 1 life. If you prefer a complete game and haven't tried Rogue Legacy 1 - that would be a good shout in the meantime. If you want to support the devs and get behind this game during early access, the game is made by a wonderful studio who are passionate about what they do and their community of players.

Detailed feedback as a someone with 80 hours in RL1
What I enjoyed
- The visual style took a little adjustment but I feel it's really pleasant to play.
- Fast forward mechanic really nicely done for cut-scenes
- The tutorial covered all the skills well, the story telling was interesting, though intentionally a little cryptic
- I really enjoyed the different classes. They add different challenges and feel to playthroughs. Barb feels very different to knight which is great considering both are melee hitters.
- Armor: it took a while to understand it, but I like how it encourages not getting hit rather than being careless and just regenerating health with runes/meat.
- Enemies: I like them, good variance and each has their "tell" for you to avoid damage.
- Heirloom challenges: these were interesting and a nice way to introduce gated / metroidvania sort of progression. Felt a shame you could only do them once, but I understand they provided a free heal so just throwing a chest at the end instead to allow replayability probably wouldn't be balanced.
- Platforms that disappear when you leave them is really nice - presenting the same type of challenge as platforms that disappear under your feet, but not punishing those with slower reactions
- I liked seeing my growing castle in the backdrop of the dock scene.
- The trails behind the spinning balls to help predict their path
- Pipe mechanic is interesting and a good addition
- Boss felt great, I enjoyed learning the tells and reacting to beat it after some attempts
- The traits were fun as usual, the incentive to play more challenging ones for bonus gold is a good idea.

How I feel after some 12+ hours in game:
I really enjoyed my experience with the game so far, and I am excited to see the game continue to get developed. I had those same moments of adrenaline, panic, focus, "oh sh!t" and laughter as I experienced in RL1.

I feel as soon as I maxed out the current skill tree and I had nothing to spend my gold on - my incentive to clear rooms lessened greatly. which made me feel a bit negative, because it felt like I wasn't playing Rogue Legacy properly. I love clearing all rooms. The chance of finding a relic is good, but the idea of popping a room and seeing "oh its just a normal room" then leaving feels like the game isn't being played naturally. I understand the balance of skill tree/progression won't be possible with only one full area, but as far as EA replayability - it does hinder my enjoyment a bit.

When I started a new save file and tried playing through as far as I could with no perks or skill tree, it felt more "rogue legacy". the epic quest to avoid taking uneccessary damage, feeling that I had to be precise to succeed. Was really rewarding, and I've been working on that as a way to still enjoy the game.
