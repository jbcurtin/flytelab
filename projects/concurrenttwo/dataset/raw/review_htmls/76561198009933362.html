When it comes to open world games, I tend to find myself bored part of the way in and never finish them. Far Cry 4 fixes a lot of the issues in the previous one, and makes the whole open world aspect even more enjoyable, to the point where I actually played through all the way until the credits rolled. And not because it's a short game - quite the opposite, as it took quite a healthy chunk of time for me to get through it.

First off, the world itself is fun to explore. There are lots of ways to get around, lots of things to see, and there are always random events going on - soldiers holding resistance fighters hostage, civilians getting attacked by animals, a military convoy driving by, etc. Thankfully you don't HAVE to do every last event, as most of the progressions in the game that are tied to random events usually just want you to do one of them to get your feet wet and at least try it out. If it turns out you like them, then there are a few guns and stuff that unlock if you do four of them, but they aren't that important. I wasn't really too fond of the armed escort missions, which just have you standing on a truck while enemies assault you, but I did enjoy having to hunt down armed convoys. As for getting around, aside from going on foot or in a car, there are little single-occupant helicopters you can fly around which are pretty fun. You also get a wingsuit early on, so any time you are at an elevated position you can just jump off and wingsuit away, ending with a parachute to slow down. All of these systems for moving around tie together in interesting ways, and I liked being able to fly over one enemy outpost lobbing grenades from a launcher in a helicopter, while wingsuiting in discreetly and stealthily taking out another. One other cool thing you can do is release a car's brakes. So if you park up an incline, you can get out, cut the brakes, and then use it as a distraction when it smashes into a machinegun nest.

I also like hearing the villain on the radio throughout the entire game. SPOILER ALERT for Far Cry 3, but killing off your memorable villain halfway through pretty much kills off any desire to keep on playing, and I stopped FC3 barely minutes into the second island. This game is all about Pagan Min. I've heard people online say he might not be as good of a villain as Vas, but you know what? I can't remember a thing about Vas besides the first time you meet him, because you barely do jack with him. Pagan is the face of the nation. You find posters with his face on them everywhere. You hear propaganda broadcasts glorifying him. He taunts you on the radio frequently. Every soldier talks about dying for his glory. Every resistance fighter curses his name.

One thing that I really hated was that you cannot pause cutscenes. What year is it? What game doesn't let you pause cutscenes? That is such a fundamental part of any modern game, it completely boggles my mind that this thing could actually get through QA without a pause feature in the cutscenes. And it's not just that you can't pause them, you can't skip them either. So did you just die during a plot mission? Hope you enjoy rewatching the 90 second cutscene. Thankfully, none of the cutscenes where this happens are too long or too frequent, as most of the real content of the game comes to you from non-cutscene dialogue, but it's still annoying. Another annoyance is that there are no subtitles for radio stations. There is an in-game radio station that has some great, hilarious content - which you often can't hear because you're too busy revving the engine and trying to get the hell away from three armed trucks, all the while getting shot at, shooting back, and triggering explosions, all of which are overpowering the radio.

I mostly play Western RPGs, so I thought I'd try getting into some more JRPGs, so bear in mind that this review is based on that perspective. If you love JRPGs already you probably don't even need a review.

Anyway, overall I think this is a great game with an interesting story and fun gameplay. On the upside, it uses an action combat system that makes it feel like you're playing a hack-and-slash instead of a clunky turn-based party system. You have "combos" that you create yourself by assigning skills to your four controller buttons (I played with a PS4 controller hooked up via bluetooth, I didn't try using my mouse and keyboard) and then use that to diversify your attacks. Basically, you have 16 skills assigned to a 4x4 grid representing the 4 buttons, and whether it's the first through fourth attack in your combo. It encourages you to shake this up all the time as you keep getting new skills, and eventually you can't use all of them at once. The story itself is a bit cliche but has enough fresh things in it to keep things lively, the specifics of which are best discovered when playing the game instead of spoiling it. The characters are also interesting and you get plenty of dialogue with them, reminiscent of the party banter in a Bioware game.

Then there's the downsides. Well, first of all, this game has graphics that would have looked outdated in 2007, let alone 2017. I installed a "fix" that could enhance some of the processing effects, but there's only so much you can do to existing models and textures. I think part of this might be due to using an archaic game engine, and another part of the reason for this is probably due to the game needing to render GIANT map areas. Which leads me to the another of the big downsides I felt this game had: the map. Holy crap. This game loves using big areas. It seems like everything requires traversing gigantic map areas that take minutes to cross if you just sprint through them (and slightly less when you get to the halfway point and gain the ability to move through maps 50% faster). But while these areas are big, they are pretty damn empty. Sure, there might be a hidden chest here or a special monster there, but 90% of the time you're just going through a low-resolution field/hallway/cave and trying to get to the other side. Aside from that there are a bunch of little interface issues that pop up indicating a lack of polish. None of those things are enough to ruin the game, but they kind of take you out of it for a moment. Little things, like the camera in the minimap not lining up with you're character's heading in the real world, or how annoying it is to deal with certain menus, or a character's hair always clipping through their hat. All in all, this is not the sort of game you play to push your video card to the limit.

Wolfenstein is, without a doubt, a great action-packed experience. The gameplay is solid, having the core essentials of a shooter down to a T, and then a cover system that is actually intuitive. The guns are pretty cool, and each gun has an alternative mode - like a pistol having a silencer or a machinegun having a rocket attachment - which when combined with the ability to dual wield basically every gun (even the sniper rifle. For some reason) lets you have some crazy gunfights.

The WWII shooter has been done to death, and The New Order manages to have a great story that avoids just using the usual cliches - it takes those typical WWII shooter action sequences you would normally expect, then twists them to hell and back through the crazy superscience Nazi scifi machine until it turns into a ridiculously fun tale.

As for the negatives, I can't believe this game doesn't have intelligent ladder-climbing. Thankfully there are very few ladders you actually have to go down (you usually go up) but for those few cases, I would always accidentally run off the ladder and take some falling damage.

So just to start off with the TLDR: I like this game, and I would recommend buying it at a sale price ($30 or less), but I really feel like it suffers from a weak first half and loads of time-wasting elements.

Now for the slightly longer opinions: The story itself is not that good. The twists and turns are highly predictable and formulaic. Not that those are inherently bad, there are plenty of games where those things can be utilized well, but in Rise of the Tomb Raider it very much feels like a slapdash combination of the plot of the first two Uncharted games sprinkled with elements of Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade. The story is, in short: Go to a snow-covered mountain, discover an ancient secret, try to prove your father was right about the holy grail - sorry, "divine source" - and all the while a crazy religious guy with a very Russian-sounding name leads a group of heavily armed mercenaries and wants you dead.

Story aside, the characters are great. Or rather, character, as Lara is the only person worth knowing about. And in that regard the game succeeds, since I care about her more than Indiana Jones or Nathan Drake. The problem with the other characters is that none of them really... do anything. From the first game, we don't have any returning characters besides Lara and Jonah. All of the time spent in the first game developing her, and she's gone. So at least this game could focus more on Jonah, right? Wrong. He's in the game so little after the tutorial/first area, we don't really learn much about him. A pair of villains are introduced, and they at least have some moderate background story revealed about them, and some of it is pretty interesting, but for the most part it boils down to the typical bad guy stuff. There are also a pair of allies Lara meets on the island, and both have the potential to be pretty cool when you first meet them, but you pretty much don't do anything with them besides get quests from them towards the end of different dungeons. If you want to learn anything about them, it has to be discovered by reading random notes laying around like it's a Dark Souls or Elder Scrolls game.

As for the gameplay itself, the game is a third-person shooter with stealth, platforming, and survival/exploration elements to it. All of those are fine enough for what they are. The combat is decent enough, and where it really shines is how you can level up and gain more abilities or ammunition for the style of play you like. Do you like using the bow? Then you can upgrade it so Lara can 'lock-on' to multiple enemies at once by aiming with it beforehand, and then shoot all of them at once. I thought that was pretty cool. Do you like stealth? You can get upgrades that let you do stealth takedowns by jumping down on someone. Again, that was pretty cool. Those are just two examples, there's more like incendiary rounds for your shotgun, or being able to do other sorts of takedowns, things like that.

Now, where the game grinds my gears is the platforming. The platforming is easily the weakest element of the game. When you start off, Lara can jump, she can climb things with her hands, she can ride down zip lines, and she can use her climbing axes to climb ice walls... which is basically identical to climbing normal walls, it's just that you press a different button to do it. And that's pretty much all you have access to for half of the game. This is agonizingly boring, and I pretty much ignored trying to do anything extra for that half of the game when it came to running and jumping, because every damned tomb and area used the same basic walls and layouts to allow Lara to go around with her limited mobility. Around a third of the way in you get rope arrows, which doesn't really help much in this regard, but it at least lets the game add more rope things to the tombs so it at least makes things slightly less monotonous. This all changes towards the second half of the game. Very quickly, Lara starts getting plenty of new things to play with. One of these is a wire spool attached to your climbing axes, which is easily my favorite upgrade, as it allows you to use it like a grappling hook. After you get this, the platforming gameplay is practically a new game. Not longer are you stuck with the super linear dungeon layout, instead you start getting a lot more moving platforms, a lot more vertical fun in the tombs (not that previous tombs lacked vertical layouts, it's just that when the only way previously to go vertical was to climb a wall, it was very limited), and most importantly, it starts giving you multiple ways to get through the same platforming puzzle.

The level design is also very inconsistent due to the limited platforming at the start. Several of the locations (especially towards the beginning) feel very uninspired, where you have to do the same basic repetitive tasks to 'solve' a basic puzzle. Then there are a few levels (especially towards the end) where the design, puzzles, and platforming are incredible. The former outweigh the latter by a significant margin. Another problem with the levels is that they incorporate a lot of "do nothing" gameplay. I cannot count how many times I was surprised find out that the game honestly expected me to hold down the forward key for a minute just to have Lara leisurely walk forward in a tomb, or stomp ahead in the snow, or shimmy along a ledge, while letting some animation or dialogue of some sort play out that I'm supposed to focus on. This is what cutscenes are supposed to be for. I gain nothing by having to sit here 'playing' the game to see this scene, rather than being able to just watch it as a cutscene instead.

So all in all, if you can grit your teeth and survive the rather middling first half of the game, the second half is a rewarding experience.

Many years ago, there was a type of game called the "dungeon crawl". They involve lots of party management, basic 4-direction movement, 2D graphics, and have a great sense of exploration. If you've played Grimrock half a decade ago, you know this sort of genre already. That said, Sakura Dungeon is one of the first good dungeon crawls I've played in years.

The party management and battle system are very reminiscent of Pokemon's triple battles, where you have three of your monsters out in the field at once, and you're required to manage the stats and moves of your active and reserve party in order to have type/elemental coverage against the wild enemies you may encounter. The art is great, and many of the dungeon floors have different aesthetics so that they each have their own "feel" to them as you progress. The game has a slight bit of grind, but for the most part you don't have to spend any time explicitly leveling up because by the time you reach the door to exit a dungeon, you'll have gotten into enough fights that you are already at a good level.

I have to admit that while I liked the first Dishonored, I was not the biggest fan. I also thought Knife of Dunwall was pretty good. But despite that, I didn't really prioritize buying Dishonored 2, and it wasn't until I got it as a birthday gift many months later that I actually got around to playing this.

♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥.

This is one of the most refreshing stealth/FPS games I've played in years. The the start, the game shakes things up by not just making you play through as Corvo and his familiar - but revamped - powers, but also to play as Emily and her own unique power set. After a brief starting/tutorial mission to get the hang of the game, you unlock your first power and get thrown into the first of several large levels. While many other games say that there are different ways to get something done, Dishonored 2 truly means it. Just in that first level alone, trying to get into a secure area has multiple different routes you can take, rather than just having one stealth option and one assault option.

The gameplay itself is great too. The combat feels fast, fluid, and pulse-pounding when you get to it, especially in a pseduo-stealth run where you're not gearing up to fight. The stealth gameplay features all sorts of ledges, vents, guard routes, and use of the environment. The powers themselves cause things to be radically different as well. Corvo has an ability that lets him possess something, like a rat, a corpse, or even a living person, which he can use for his own unique stealth approaches. Emily meanwhile has the ability to 'link' targets together, up to four at the max upgrade, and have what happens to one target happen to all the other linked targets as well. This opens up some really fun navigating of the levels as you sneak around trying to link a few people, then jump on one guard and bash his head into the ground, causing three other guards to collapse simultaneously.

Movement in the game is also very good. You have your standard array of parkour abilities, and you can get upgrades (which I highly recommend) that let you jump a dozen feet high, or survive massive falls. Corvo has the simple Blink ability, which functions like a teleport, but can be chained with his other abilities. Emily doesn't have that, instead having the Far Reach power that lets her shoot some sort of weird spiritual grappling hook at a ledge, and yank her towards it. The upgrade paths further the difference between these two, as while Corvo gains the ability to be even more stealthy while teleporting around, Emily's Far Reach gains the ability to yank other things towards her - including unsuspecting guards. Once I had that ability, I wasted a lot of time in the rest of the game engineering the perfect situations for her to be able to yank a guard towards her, screaming loudly all the way towards her, and then choke them out and put their unconscious body in a bathroom.

On a related note, if you play as Emily and yank a guard towards her, they scream VERY loudly all the way towards her. As the other guards have ears, it can be quite a mess.

And then nearly each level shakes things up by having its own unique gimmick. I will avoid spoiling anything, but in a nutshell there is a level that involves a unique environmental effect, a level that features a constantly-shifting map, a level where there is a lot of vertical connection between everything, and then my favorite one, which I can't actually describe without spoiling the whole mission.

All in all, a great game with a lot of heart, gumption, and replay value.
