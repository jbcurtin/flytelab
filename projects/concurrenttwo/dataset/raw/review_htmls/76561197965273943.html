Gist: It isn't actually an escape room/card game/rogue-like, and the advertising is wholly dishonest.

This was a disappointing one. The first 1/3 of the game has a great concept, really tight mechanics, stellar atmosphere and this really interesting blend of escape-room/roguelite genres. This is the part of game that is advertised. If the game has ran with this and developed it, it would probably be one of my favourites. However, the game that the marketing shows you is only about 1/3 of the total. As you progress, the gameplay alters completely and deteriorates in favour of unimpressive meta elements ("what if the enemy cards where named after your steam friends?!") and mechanics are swapped for less developed ones. The game is also way too easy (and constantly gets easier) which means that the more promising mechanics don't even require you to engage with them (I'm tasking about conduits and gems mainly, for those who have played it). I think the game director may have even realised all of this because one character lampshades it by telling you to stick with the "hackneyed" mechanics. The concept of the story is interesting but perhaps as should be expected the ending only ties into an ARG without much real conclusion to the intrigue of the plot.

Overall it seems like this game got carried away with itself by thinking that it was more avant-garde that it really is, and relying on the player being wowed enough that they didn't notice or mind that the game they had bought and sat down to play had been a front for a "ludo-narrative art experience" or whatever you want to call it. In reality, the meta wow-factor for which Inscryption sacrifices its gameplay and the expectations of the audience doesn't stand up in an age were most people have played Undertale. I'd give act 1 (the advertised spooky card game) 10/10, act 2 6/10 and act 3 4/10.

The first half of this game is great, but it falters after that. Amongst other things, the combat gets a bit stale and the atmosphere of the earlier game is lost. Xen is really tilting.

There are a good few bugs. For example, I couldn't see my health or energy unless I started the game unless I started the game with the launch command "-console". My save was also wiped at one point (not a massive deal since you can start a new game from the start of any chapter, but still). The last lewels of the game also ran pretty badly.

The game suffers from the source engine physics obsession which means you will be killed by props and lifts if you look at them the wrong way, and you can get stuck on/bounce off pretty much anything larger than a nat's nipple. Especially annoying on chase sequences and the frequent jumping puzzles.

The game's difficulty is set to the hardest by default. At this difficulty, most of the combat mechanics are unbalanced, so I'd recommend turning it down to the middle at least. Otherwise you'll be dealing with bullet sponge enemies taking off 30% of your hp from across the map with their hitscan weapons. You'll also get into plenty of situations where you have only a sliver of HP for an upcoming combat arena with no way to recover it (this happens on any difficulty, but turning it down helps stop the perpetual load-get one shot-load loop).

Despite the frustration, it's a good game (it's half life after all). Just don't go into it thinking you're getting any sort of polished experience.
