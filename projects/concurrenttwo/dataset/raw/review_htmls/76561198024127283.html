Recommended
288.7 hrs on record (4.8 hrs at review time)
I picked this up during the Steam Summer Sale, so I currently don't have a lot of time in the game, but I have enjoyed the time I have played, and I will be playing more. A longer review will (probably) be written in due course, but here are the main points as I see them:

Pros:
* Graphically nice, even with some clipping issues
* Very charming and light hearted dialogue
* The tutorial is well paced and fairly thorough. It doesn't just drop you in the game and say "off you go then"
* The Zoopedia is quite informative, so there's a nice educational element to the game
* The amount of customisation you can do when building and constructing is astounding. The Workshop for Planet Zoo is a great feature because of this broad functionality; you are largely limited by your imagination.
* When building and placing objects the game is somewhat "smart" and helpful


Cons:
* The graphics only look really good at the higher settings but that does slow the game down considerably on older (>3year old) hardware when your zoo gets to be a medium-to-large size
* The Zoopedia is wrong in places. For instance, it put Mali and ♥♥♥♥♥ in the wrong place in Africa
* The build/construction interface sometimes could do with being a bit "dumb" rather than "smart"
* While the game is geared to be quite relaxing, I find myself becoming a little stressed out after a couple of hours of game play. This is quite probably more a commentary on me than the game, though.

Not Recommended
1,496.1 hrs on record (1,111.2 hrs at review time)
I have a lot of hours in Stellaris, the vast majority of them before the release of the Megacorp expansion. Prior to Magacorp my recommendation would have been a resounding yes. However, 14 months on from Megacorp release, the game is still fundamentally flawed and in many ways broken. Ultimately, I cannot recommend this game.

################ Before Megacorp ################

Prior to the release of Megacorp, planets had a tile-based system of population growth and building construction. This was pretty basic, but it was functional. It was also pretty clear and straightforward to manage. You built buildings on tiles, pops grew on tiles. If you had multiple species on a given planet pops from all species would grow, but at a reduced rate.

During this period the economy was pretty basic, revolving around energy credits, minerals, and scientific research. There were some special resources, but these were not enormously important and had a certain amount of RNG to their spawn locations. Overall, this meant there was a simplicity to the economy, which was pretty easy to manage, even for large, sprawling empires.

The net result, however, was you spent only some of your time fiddling with planets, and more time on working out how to conquer the galaxy. The big picture was more prominent.

Some parts of the Stellaris community complain about the old system in two ways. First, the economy was too simplistic and it needed more "depth". Second, capturing planets in war resulted in a reasonable amount of micromanagement on the newly acquired planets, while you organise which buildings should be built in which tile, and if you were a synthetic empire, which synthetic pops to build on which tiles.

Tangential to this, there were also some complaints about game lag and slowdown, as the end game approached.

################ Design of Megacorp ################

During 2018, Paradox was seemingly listening to these complaints and aimed to address them. With the release of Megacorp, the tile-based planet system was gone in favour of something else, and the economy was being made more complex with the introduction of secondary industries which created special resources.

The publicly announced ambition of these changes were: to make the mid game more interesting; make special resources more useful and predictable; add complexity to the economy; reduce micromanagement on planets; and to improve game lag and performance.

################ The reality ################

Unfortunately, all of the promises and ambitions of Megacorp have been almost entirely missed. While the new system does make the special strategic resources more interesting and more predictable, everything else is a miss.

Micromanagement with the new planetary district system is far worse than before. Pops tend to jump around the different building and district jobs available, which makes placing a given pop in a job you want impossible without a significant amount of micromanagement. Moreover, if you have multiple species on a planet, which pop starts growing is somewhat random. Previously, every pop grew with a reduced rate. Now one pop grows at full rate but you have no control over which pop grows. This is surprisingly annoying for Synthetic empires.

The economy is somewhat problematic in that you can often find yourself chasing your tail to a certain extent. It also makes developing planets and your empire incredibly slow. The root of some of this is that pops are now stratified, essentially into working class, middle class, ruling class. When a job opens up at a higher tier, a lower tier pop is promoted to take that job. So if you open up a new alloy refinery (middle class jobs), a lower class pop leaves their current job to work in the refinery. The lower class jobs are all primary industries - energy credits, minerals and food - and so you can find your economy temporarily going backwards as you develop planets and open up more middle class jobs. As a consequence, you need to micromanage, every planet, all the time, with every building.

However, worst of all, the game now lags so much worse than it has ever done. Even at the start of a brand new map, the game has more lag, and upon launch of Megacorp, the game was literally unplayable for it.

Even now, end game lag remains a significant problem. Come 1st of January of later years (maybe 2300 onwards, depending on PC, number of alien empires, and size of galaxy) the game will hang for between 5 and 15 seconds, while it computes a whole load of stuff. By the time you get to this point, the game is grinding along so slowly it makes it virtually impossible to play.

################ Conclusion ################

Paradox have had well over a year to address the lag issue. Last November a Redditor did some experiments and found out precisely why the game lags; there is a fundamental flaw in the implementation of the district and pop job system. Every time there is a vacant job, the game runs calculations to see if another arrangement of the pops can yield more income. However, this ends up with pops hopping around, jobs filling and vacating, flicking on and off like the lights in a slot machine.

There is really no excuse for the game to continue to be in the current state. For a lot of people, this lag will literally, literally, make the game unplayable.

But rather than address the issues of economy balance of buildings, fix the issues with micromanagement, and fix the game lag, they've been working on more DLC to fleece senseless the loyal playerbase, with the next DLC due shortly.

Overall, the game is now not worth the money or the hassle. It's a real shame for me, as Stellaris was for a while my favourite game. I obsessed over it, and had an enormous amount of fun. Megacorp destroyed the fun, and Paradox are not interested in fixing their broken game.

Right now, the only great thing about the game is the soundtrack by Andreas Waldetoft, which is totally amazing and perfect for the game. However, you don't need the game for the soundtrack: it is currently available for free on Spotify.
