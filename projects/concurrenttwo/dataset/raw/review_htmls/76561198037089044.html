My first playthrough with my main character is finally over and it took me approximately 88 hours. The counter in-game says so, but I spent some of it afk or helping others in coop. However even if we lower it all the way to 80, that's still a lot and makes this one of the longest Fromsoft titles I've played to date. My thoughts on the game are rather conflicted though. There is certainly a lot to like about Elden Ring, but it also has a lot of the same issues as some of the previous Fromsoft titles, in addition to new ones.

Positives

Exploration is very fun. There is a lot to find in the game world, and almost every dungeon or ruin has a useful reward in it, and even if the boss doesn't reward you with a useful item, you might find something useful in the dungeon itself.

The locations are quite varied. You've got a land afflicted by disease, a decrepit castle, keep in the middle of a poisonous swamp, a manor in the middle of a volcano, an ancient city torn apart by the elements, and many others. All of them have unique threats and environmental hazards to deal with, which ensures that the player never gets bored.

The game is pretty. While at a closer look it is a bit dated, artdesign carries the game.

There's a big variety of weapons, armors and spells. In addition to the new Ashes of War system (basically weapon arts from DS3) this lets the player customize their character to a level we've never seen before. There are also fewer redundant items than in previous titles made by the developer. Almost every weapon or tool has their use, and shields are once again a viable option for defence after being butchered in Dark Souls 3.

Negatives

The game is extremely stingy with consumable items. There are very few antidotes, buff and debuff items you can find in the world, most of them have to be purchased or crafted manually. The issue is that the player has to find special items to unlock new recipes or expand merchant stock. While this wasn't much of an issue in previous titles, the map in ER is much larger than before, with materials being spread all around it. As a result, it's perfectly possible for the player to run out of useful upgrade materials or crafting resources, or to be unable to craft an important item. I beat the entire game and explored a lot of the map, But I never found a way to craft or buy the cure for Crimson Rot. Some might say that I should've been more dedicated in my exploration, but I think expecting the players to scour every inch of the map is unreasonable.

The devs constantly reuse assets, and overall there's a lot of repeating content and not enough new stuff to find. As an example, there's a sidequest available early on in a castle located to the South. Beastmen there rebelled and are in the process of killing everyone in the castle. Daughter of the captain of the guard asks you to bring him a letter ushering him to come to her, since he sent her away but the convoy has been ambushed. You go through the castle and meet the father. You progress through the castle and eventually meet the alpha beastman that probably started the rebellion. You kill it and that's that. A short, self-contained story located in an interesting location. However, this same boss is also reused multiple times later on. While his use as a mob is annoying but ultimately understandable, him appearing again as a boss at least once is very annoying, but that's nothing compared to a pair of bosses who appear approximately 6 times throughout the game, with one of those encounters being in the main story. Almost every location is also filled with soldiers. While they have one or two different moves, for the most part their movesets are very similar if not identical, and the only visual difference is the coat of arms they have on their armor. Catacombs and ruins also look nearly identical most of the time, and almost never have any interesting gimmicks in them, instead simply requiring the player to get to the end and pull a lever (in catacombs) or find a basement and kill the boss (optional) and loot the treasure (in ruins). While it's understandable that an open world game would have some reused assets, it's very disappointing to see from Fromsoftware. They even reuse some things from their previous titles. It might be an effective use of their resources, but that doesn't make it look any less lazy to me.

The boss and enemy quality overall fluctuates constantly. There are some amazing or at least interesting enemy and boss designs, but there's also a lot enemies that don't seem to have much thought poured into them. The game also relies a lot on group fights or duo bosses. That's not to say that those fights can't be fun, but a lot of situations feel like they've been designed with the sole purpose of making the game difficult rather than making the fight interesting. Reception to General Radahn has been mixed, but at least the developers have tried something new with him. Pitting the player against two powerful enemies or a boss surrounded by minions and ganking them is simply lazy and can screw over some builds pretty easily. Enemies and bosses also have very high tracking, The highest I've seen since Dark Souls 2. Dark Souls 2 had tracking to such an extent it became a meme, but one thing I can say about it is that I don't remember enemies tracking the player in mid-air to such an extent that they wouldn't just turn around mid-air, but change their entire trajectory mid-flight . I did, however, see this in Elden Ring, and I've seen it happen more than once. Some enemies and bosses are even capable of doing a complete 180 in an instant just to hit the player. While I understand that circle strafing and backstab fishing have both been very popular strategies and developers had to change something, I don't think that making every enemy spin like they're standing on top of a record player is a good idea. Why not give the enemies moves to deal with those things? Dark Souls 2 had an enemy that could fall on their back to flatten the player, and in Elden Ring Crucible Knight has a move where it quickly hits the player with its tail. If you are trying to focus on his back, you will get hit by it as it comes out, which forces the player to be more careful and either engage the enemy head-on, or try and stay to the side of the opponent. Why not give more enemies such moves?

The camera is abysmal against large enemies. I don't even have anything else to say about it, it has been an issue since the series first began, and the only title where camera was almost fine is Sekiro since every enemy there was more or less the same size as the player and didn't have moves that messed with the camera too much (some situations like Lone Swordsman in the well not withstanding).

Performance is still questionable.

While I certainly would be lying if I said that I disliked Elden Ring or didn't have fun with it, it still has a lot of the issues that Fromsoft have been plagued with since Dark Souls 2. It feels like after the huge success of Dark Souls 1 they've been focused on making a difficult, combat-focused game first and foremost at the expense of everything else that made Dark and Demon Souls unique. I would like to give a neutral rating if I could, but since it doesn't exist I'll give it a positive one, only because it would be downright hypocritical of me to give it a negative review after enjoying it for more than 100 hours. I really hope that they re-evaluate what made Dark Souls 1 and Demon Souls the successes that they were, but considering the overwhelming player support their games have been garnering primarily due to said difficulty and combat, I doubt that will happen. Still, Elden Ring is a good action game, so if you only care about the gameplay and nothing else, give it a go.
